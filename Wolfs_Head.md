# WOLF'S HEAD

There is a stretch of the old road south of Jórvík where the trees close over the track and the light comes down green and cold even at midday, and it is here, in the autumn of his twenty-fifth year, that a man walks alone toward a debt he does not want to pay.

He keeps to the trees at the road's edge, not on the road itself. He has done this so long it is no longer caution but posture, the way other men slouch or stand straight. He makes no sound when he walks. This is not a gift. It is ten years of practice by a man for whom being heard has meant, more than once, being hunted. His feet find the quiet ground the way another man's tongue finds a missing tooth — without thought, drawn to the gap.

He has a name. His father gave him one and his mother gave him another, in secret, and he has spent his life unsure which of the two is true. To the few who know what he is, he has no name at all. He is *vargr* — wolf's head, the word the law uses for a man it has cast out, a man any other may kill the way one kills a wolf, without answer, without price. That is what he is, before he is anything else. Everything he does, he does because of it.

---

## The two names

He was got in violence and he knows it, which is a thing few men know about themselves and fewer can carry.

Before he was born, a fleet came up out of the grey water onto an English shore, and a Norse warrior named Steinarr took a Christian woman by force and left with the tide, carrying nothing away with him and leaving everything behind. The woman was gentle and she did not survive what he had done, not truly — she carried the child but the carrying used her up, and she died in the straw the night he came, with her own sister's hands on her.

That sister was called Eda. She was a freedwoman of the English church-folk, small and stubborn and full of a faith she was not always allowed to name aloud. When her sister died she took the child up and made a decision that would cost her the rest of her life: she raised him as her own, and let him believe it. For the first years of his life he was simply her son, an English boy in an English place, and the word *father* meant nothing because there was no father, only Eda and the goats and the low church where she prayed when the men were away.

She had him baptized. This was no small thing and no safe one; she did it in secret, in cold water, with a borrowed priest who was himself half a slave, and she gave him at that font a name out of her own tongue — **Oswine**, *friend of God* — and she told him, when he was old enough to hold a secret, that this was his true name, the one Heaven knew him by, and that no one else must ever hear it.

Then Steinarr came back.

He had not forgotten the shore. He learned, on his return, that there was a boy, and that the boy was his, and men like Steinarr do not leave a son in another man's country. But this time he did not simply take. He had grown clever with age. He made a bargain with a local strongman, a man with no scruples and a want of Norse steel at his back, and among the terms of that bargain was a delivery of Christian women out of a small religious house — thralls, to be carried north across the water. Eda was among them. The boy went with her, because she would not let him go, and because a child is easy to fold into a cargo of the taken.

On the crossing Steinarr came to them once. He told Eda plainly that the past was to stay buried in the sea behind them. If she ever spoke of the shore, of the sister, of how the boy came to be, he would kill them both, and no one aboard would lift a hand, because to the men on that ship a thrall's life weighed about what a thrall cost — eight marks of silver, less than a good sword. She understood. She was English and Christian and property, three things worth nothing in a Norse assembly, and she had learned long ago exactly how much her word could buy.

So the boy grew up Norse. Steinarr gave him a Norse name — **Eskil** — the way a man burns his mark into a beast he means to keep, and set about making him into something useful. And the boy answered to it, and learned the northern tongue until it sat in his mouth as easily as the southern one, and became, without meaning to, the one thing the frontier prized and he would come to hate: a man of two tongues, at home in neither.

Two names, then. Eskil, that his father branded on him. Oswine, that his mother breathed into him in cold water. He has never known which one is the lie.

---

## The vow

Eda was not a saint, though he sometimes tries to remember her as one. She was tired and frightened for most of the years he knew her, and her God gave her more fear than comfort in that pagan household, and she was not always kind, because kindness is expensive when you are owned. But she loved him with the whole of whatever was left in her, and she taught him the shape of a mercy the north had no use for.

When he was near sixteen she began to die. It was a wasting sickness, slow, and she knew where it was taking her, and knowing gave her a courage she had never been able to afford. In her last days she told him the truth — all of it. The shore. Her sister. The father who was not returning from a raid to a home but returning, always, to the scene of a crime he had never once thought to name a crime. She told him whose son he really was and why she had lied, and she asked his forgiveness for the lie, which he gave without understanding yet what it would cost him to know.

Steinarr came back from that raid to find his household unmade. Whether he learned that the woman had talked, or only came home drunk and looking for a place to put his anger, the boy never knew. There was shouting, and then there was the father's hand on the dying woman, and then the boy was between them, which he had sworn to himself he would one day be.

They fought. It was not a duel and there was nothing noble in it; it was two bodies in a small dark room breaking whatever they fell against. They went through the shutter together into the yard. The boy landed badly and could not rise. His father landed worse. His head found the hearth-stone that sat by the door, and the sound it made was not loud, and then there was no more Steinarr in the world, only the thing he had been, cooling in the frost.

He killed his father. He wants that plainly said, because he says it to himself plainly every day. Even in defense, even at sixteen, even a man like Steinarr — he took a life, and no accounting since has ever let him put it down.

The strangest part he has never solved. Eda dragged herself down to the yard. She went first to her son, to feel for his breath, and finding it, she left him — and went to the body of the man who had bought her, broken her country's women, threatened her life, and made her a liar for a decade. She knelt by Steinarr in the cold and she wept. Real grief, the kind that has no audience. The boy watched her mourn his father and could make no sense of it then and can make none of it now, and that unsolved thing has become a small permanent stone in him: that a good woman could weep for a monster, and that he will never be able to ask her why.

She died days later. Her last words to him were not a blessing but an order, and he has spent his life obeying its letter because he was never sure of its spirit.

*Never become what he was,* she told him. *Never become an evil man.* And then, more terribly, the thing he seized on and could not afterward let go of: *Never take a life. Not even the life of the cruelest man who ever drew breath. Death is not ours to give. Only God may take. You are not God. You are my son, and you will obey me in this as a child obeys.*

He was sixteen and broken and grieving and he took the last sentence of a dying woman and made of it a law with no mercy in it. *Never take a life.* He built the rest of his years on those four words, on their narrowest possible reading, and only much later did he come to suspect that she had not meant a rule at all. She had meant *be good.* She had meant *don't let him win by making you into him.* She had given him a whole heart to carry and he had chosen instead to carry a single unbending line, because a line is easier to hold than a heart, and because if he ever set it down he was afraid of what he would become.

## Solveig, and the sounds

There was one other person in the world who knew him, and she was already old when he was a boy.

Solveig was a freedwoman who kept the settlement's goats and did the small ritual work no one important wanted to do — a woman on the edge of everything, sharp-tongued, fond of ale, carrying an old hatred of the Christians for something done to her long before he was born, which she never explained and he never asked. She was not a warm woman. She would as soon cuff him as speak to him. But she saw the strange, silent, watchful boy that the settlement had already decided was wrong, and instead of turning away she gave him the one thing that was hers to give.

She made sounds. All the goatherds did, after a fashion — the calls and clicks and low throat-noises that move stock across a hillside, that soothe a frightened animal, that carry across a valley where a shout would not. But Solveig had made an art of it in her long solitary years, mouth-music, a whole language of it: the click of a tongue that became the fall of hooves, a hum from deep in the chest that a startled animal would answer, the trick of breathing in and out through shaped sounds so that the noise never stopped for breath. She could make the coming of rain. She could make a fox laugh. She taught him all of it, patiently, as the only thing she had worth passing on, and it was the first patience anyone had ever shown him.

He learned it the way he learned everything — completely, obsessively, past the point of use. It became, before he understood what it was becoming, the way he held himself together. When the two worlds in him ground too hard against each other, when his father's blood in him woke up wanting things his mother's law forbade, he would go where no one was and lose himself in the sounds until the wanting quieted. It was not magic. It was closer to the way a man will hum without noticing on a long walk, except that for him the humming was a wall he built between himself and what was inside him.

Solveig died while he was gone. He had left on a long southern journey — for silver, for work, for the moving-on that had already become his sickness — and he had known she was failing and he had gone anyway, told himself she had years, told himself the work mattered. When he came back to the settlement months later she was already under the ground and no one could tell him much about the end of her. He did not choose to be absent for his mother's death; he had chosen to be absent for Solveig's, and the difference between those two guilts is the difference between a wound and a wound picked open by your own hand. He carries the second one worse.

Now the sounds are all he has left of her, and they have turned, over the years, into something that is killing him by inches in three separate ways.

They comfort him, and so they keep him alone, because he cannot make them where anyone can hear. They are the only voice he has for his grief, and so certain patterns — the ones she made over graves — he can no longer make at all; three times he has tried the burial-song she taught him and three times it has broken him down into a weeping he cannot stop, once close enough to a settlement that people heard, and told each other afterward what they had always suspected about him. And the throat that makes them is not what it was. Push the sounds too long and his voice goes for a day, two days, gone entirely, and there is old scar-stiffness in there from the years he did not know his limits, so that even his ordinary speaking voice now carries a permanent low rasp, as though he is always just recovering from shouting he never did.

But the worst of it is not the cost to him. It is what the sounds mean to everyone else.

There is no word, in his world, for *a man who makes strange noises because a lonely goatherd taught him to.* The category does not exist. And so both faiths reach for the only words they have, and both words are death.

To the pagans it is *seiðr* — the old shaping-magic, prophecy and trance and the calling of spirits, women's work, and worse than women's work in a man's mouth. A man who does *seiðr* is *ergi*, unmanned, shameful in a way that in the north can justify a blade. He has been named *seiðr* more times than he can count. A jarl once tried to have him killed for it and he ran and did not stop running for a week. A *völva*, a seeress protecting her own trade, stood up in a market and told the crowd he was stealing the voices of the dead, and cursed him, and he felt the crowd turn the way a herd turns before it stampedes, and he left that region and never went back. Children have thrown stones at him across a field, screaming the word for witch, while he sat by a stream making the sound of rain for no one but himself.

To the Christians it is the Devil. Sounds that do not come from a human throat come from something else wearing a human throat; the animal noises are worst, because everyone knows what shapes the fallen ones take. A priest once decided to save him. He came at him with two men and holy water and words, to cast the thing out, and it was not a metaphor and it was not gentle, and the man who does not kill had to hurt three men and break out of a church to keep his life, and add another town to the long list of towns he can never enter again.

So he makes his sounds alone, briefly, never in a settlement, and stops the instant anyone comes. The one gift a dying woman gave him is the one thing about him most likely to get him burned. He has understood for years that Solveig, who had no faith and no country and no love from anyone, gave him something purer than his mother's law: she gave it to him because it was beautiful and it was hers, and she asked nothing back. It is the only thing anyone ever gave him free. And the world has decided it is proof he should die.

## The body, and the cost of it

He is built long and lean, and men underestimate him clothed, and he has let them, because being underestimated has kept him alive.

He was a thin, quick child who ran on the balls of his feet and could hold a crouch until his legs might have belonged to a stone, watching animals, watching men, watching everything. His father tried to make a warrior of him on the northern pattern — mass, and a shield, and an axe — and failed, because the boy's body would not go that way and the boy's hands would not close on the axe. What he built instead he built out of watching and out of being beaten. He has no master and never had one. He learned by taking apart every fighting man he ever saw — Saxon, Norse, Irish, whoever the raids threw up in front of him — and then by drilling the pieces alone against posts and trees and the empty air, for years, until they became his. It is a real skill and it is a crippled one. A man with no partner to train against learns to hit a thing that does not hit back, and the holes in that learning are wide, and good men have found them. He knows this about himself. He is not the perfect weapon the frightened make him out to be. He is a self-taught thing with gaps, who has survived mostly by refusing the fights that would kill him.

And the body has begun to send him the bill.

The low, hard leanness that makes him fast is the same leanness that has no fat to spare, and in the wet English cold that is not a small thing — it is the central fact of half his year. A heavier man can sleep a night in the open and wake stiff. He cannot; the cold gets into him faster and does not let go, and a bad night can kill him where it would only trouble another. This one weakness rules his whole life from October to March. It is why the wolf's-head who trusts no one must, every autumn, go and find a farm — remote enough to ask no questions, poor enough to need a winter's meat and a night-guard against wolves and men — and offer his labor for a corner of a barn and a share of the pot. Never the house. Always the barn; he is a hired danger, not a guest. And when the thaw comes in March he goes, before anyone has decided to look too hard at his face, because to be recognized is to be a corpse. In the worst years there is no farm and he goes south into milder Wessex on a three-week road through country that hates his northern look, or he tries to keep a fire alive alone in a forest for ninety nights, which is a way of dying that takes its time.

He has nearly died of the cold twice. Once at seventeen, caught by an early storm, forced to beg shelter from an English family and then to work himself half to death for weeks to pay off a charity he could not bear to owe. Once at twenty-one, ribs broken in a fall, unable to reach the shelter he'd arranged, holed up in a cave with the injury and a fever and the starvation all arriving at once, saved only because a hunter came through and found him and did not leave him there.

That hunter's name was Ketil. Remember him.

The rest of the bill is written on him in scars. A knee that aches before rain and buckles if he asks too much of it. A rib that set crooked and pulls when he twists. A hand that no longer fully closes, from a bone that healed wrong with no one to set it. He is twenty-five and he moves like a man of forty in the mornings, and the beautiful economy of him — the thing that once let him fight for as long as a fight needed — now has a bottom to it that it did not used to have. He is a tool worn well past its warranty, kept in service because there is no one to retire it and nothing to retire it to.

## What happens when he fights

There is a shape to it, and it frightens him more than it frightens anyone watching.

At the start he is cold. This is the part he trusts. He stays just outside the reach of whatever the other man is carrying, and he does not attack, he *reads* — the weight on the feet, the tell in the shoulder, the breath. He would rather learn a man than hit him. Against a knife or an axe in a steady hand he expects, honestly, to be cut, and often is, and the only way he lives is to close the distance in the half-beat when the weapon is committed to a swing and cannot come back, or to take the whole thing to the ground or into the dark where reach means less. He avoids far more fights than he takes. The fights he takes are mostly against men worse than himself — drunks, lone brigands, the desperate — and even those leave marks.

But if a fight runs long, or if there are too many of them, or if he is hurt and the fear turns over into something older, he loses himself. The cold watching drops away and the thinking drops away and something takes the wheel that is fast and sure and does not decide, only does. And the sounds come out of him then whether he wills them or not — the growls, the broken rhythms, the animal noise rising out of his chest — and this is the thing he is ashamed of, because it is the exact opposite of the silence he has built his survival on. The man who can move through a wood without waking a bird becomes, in the deep of a fight, a thing that announces itself to everyone within a mile, that cannot hide, that can be tracked by ear alone. His discipline and his loss of it live in the same body and cannot both be true at once, and it takes him a long time to come back down afterward, shaking, half-somewhere-else, dangerous to anyone who touches him before he knows them again. He has learned to be alone the moment a fight ends. It is not for their sake. It is because he does not yet know, in that first while, that he will not keep going.

He does not fight because he must. He fights because he needs to, the way another man needs drink, and he knows the difference, and he cannot stop. It is the one appetite of his father's that lived on in him, and every time he feeds it he is proving the thing he most fears about himself.

## The ledger

For a long time he believed the vow was a wall between him and his father. Lately he has begun to understand that it is a trick he plays on a scale that does not care.

He does not kill. He is very careful. He chokes a man into the dark, or breaks the joint that ends the fight, or leaves him down and walks away, and he tells himself — his mother's four words, held up like a shield — *I did not take a life.* And it is true, in the room, in the moment. It is a lie everywhere else.

Because a man he chokes and leaves in the frost, in this country, in these years, does not always wake, or does not always wake in time to keep the cold from finishing what he started. A joint he breaks does not heal clean in a world without physicians; the wound fevers, and the fever takes the man a week later in some barn where he cannot see it happen. A man he beats down and leaves helpless on a road is simply left for the next hard man to come along, and the next hard man is under no vow at all. He has begun, only recently, to do the arithmetic he spent years refusing to do, and the sum of it is this: he is almost certainly a killer many times over, at one remove, by patience and by weather and by the hands of others — and the only thing his vow has ever truly protected is his own idea of himself.

There was a farmstead, three winters back, that showed him the bottom of it. Two outlaws had killed the farmer and had a knife at the throat of his small daughter, and they told him to kill one of them or the girl would die. He did not kill. He put them both down without it, and it should have been the proof that the vow could hold even there — except that while his hands were closing the last man's throat he felt himself not stopping. He wanted the pulse to end under his fingers. He wanted it the way he wants a fight, the way his father must have wanted things, and he was a breath from it and he pulled back not out of goodness but out of the rule, the bare rule, the line he cannot cross because it is the only thing left that tells him where he ends and Steinarr begins. He kept the vow. He did not keep it because he is good. He is not sure, anymore, that he is good. He kept it the way a drowning man keeps hold of a rope: not because the rope means anything, but because his hands will not open.

That is the truth he walks around inside now, heavier than the outlawry, heavier than the cold. Not *am I like my father* — he has made his peace with fearing that. The new thing is worse. It is: *the promise I have ruined my life to keep may be a fiction, and I may have been dealing death the whole time by the slow means, and my clean hands may be the biggest lie I ever told.* He does not know what a man is supposed to do with that. He keeps walking, which is the only answer he has ever had for anything.

## The frontier, and the wealth he will not spend

The world he moves through runs on weighed silver, not counted coin. Men carry little scales and cut their arm-rings and their foreign coins into scraps to make an exact weight, and they test what they are given — a bite to feel for softness, a nick to see the color under the surface — because half of what changes hands is cut with lead and copper by someone hoping you won't look. He is cheated in this constantly. He cannot read, he cannot haggle, and — this is the part the sharp traders never guess — he does not fight for the last scrap because he does not care enough about silver to fight for it. A man walking toward the thing he most dreads does not drive a hard bargain. He takes the low weight and the bad metal and moves on, and lets them think him a fool, which is safer anyway than letting them think him worth remembering.

He carries one true fortune and he will not spend it. In this frontier, where the northern tongue and the English tongue grind together into something new in every market and every mixed household, a man who speaks both cleanly is worth more than a sword — an interpreter, a broker, the one who can stand between a Norse trader and a Saxon farmer and make the deal that neither could make alone. He could live well on his two mouths. He never has. Because to be the man who speaks for others is to be needed, and to be needed is to be *placed*, kept, known, findable — and every one of those is a rope around a wolf's-head's neck. His mother's people made him bilingual and his father's people made him hunted, and so the gift and the danger are the same gift, and he keeps the most valuable thing about himself folded up and hidden, and lets men cheat him in a language he could correct them in, and says nothing.

And the world he hides in is ending, though he is slow to feel it. The old raiding life is settling into farms and towns. The Arab silver is drying up and the trade is turning toward Christian Europe, and the network of that trade increasingly has no room in it for a pagan, so men who would never have knelt are taking the *prima signatio*, the half-conversion that lets a heathen do business with Christians without giving up his gods — the same workshops casting Thor's hammers and Christ's crosses off the same molds, merchants wearing both for insurance, whole families keeping two faiths at once out of plain good sense. His absolute vow, his all-or-nothing, was built for a world where the line between his mother's mercy and his father's violence was a bright hard thing. That world is blurring at the edges while he watches, and he does not know what his promise even means in a country where everyone is quietly becoming everything, and the wandering unkilling wolf's-head is a shape of man the age is preparing to have no further use for.

---

## The debt

Which brings him back to the road south of Jórvík, and to Ketil.

He owes the hunter his life — the plain, whole, un-metaphored fact of it, the breath in his body, given back to him in a cave two winters ago by a man under no obligation to stop. And in the north there is no heavier thing to carry than a life-debt unpaid. It is not gratitude. It is a kind of ownership. As long as the debt stands, some part of him belongs to Ketil, is answerable to Ketil, is *held* — and being held is the one thing he has spent every year of his outlawry running from, ever since his father branded a name on him like a mark on a beast. He cannot be free while he is owed. So he is going to find the man and discharge it and be no one's again.

He suspects what waits for him, and the suspicion is why his feet are slow on a road he chose. A hunter on this frontier, an aging one, does not usually call in a life-debt for silver or for labor. He calls it in because there is a man who needs killing — a feud, a raider, a wrong that honor says must be answered with blood — and there is no one else he trusts to do it. That is the shape of these debts. And so the one payment that would satisfy the north and set him free is very likely to be the one coin his mother's four words forbid him to spend. To keep the vow is to stay owned, to stay a thing that belongs to another man. To pay the debt is to become, at last, in the plainest possible way, exactly what he killed his father for being.

He does not know which he will do. This is not the same as not knowing who he is. He knows exactly who he is: a wolf's-head with two names and a dead woman's rule and another dead woman's gift, walking on a bad knee toward the one man who can free him, carrying the growing suspicion that his clean hands were never clean, into a room where he will be asked for blood. He has one question and he is walking straight at it. That is more direction than he has had in ten years, and it terrifies him, because a question you are walking toward is a question you will eventually have to answer.

---

At night, when the fire is small and there is no one for miles and it is safe, he sometimes makes one of Solveig's sounds — not the grave-song, never that, only some small living thing, the rain, or a fox, or the far-off fall of hooves that never come. And sometimes, after, in the dark, he says the two names over to himself the way another man might tell his beads.

*Eskil.* The one his father burned into him.

*Oswine.* The one his mother breathed into him in cold water. *Friend of God* — to a man who does not know if there is a God, or if the vow he has bled his whole life to keep is obedience to Heaven or only a debt to a dead woman, which may be the same thing, or may be nothing at all.

He does not know which name is true. He has never known. But he notices, on the nights he is most tired and least guarded, that it is the second one he says last, and holds onto, and carries with him down into what sleep an outlaw is allowed.

Then the fire dies, and the cold comes for him faster than it comes for other men, and before first light he is up and moving south again — quiet, unweaponed, unforgiven, and not yet caught — toward Ketil, and the question, and whatever he is going to turn out to be.
