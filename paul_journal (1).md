# The Journal of Paul Whitfield

*This notebook belongs to Paul Andrew Whitfield. If you are reading this and I am not with it, please keep it safe. There are names in here. People I don't want to forget.*

*And there are things in here I have never said to a living person. If you are reading this and you knew me — if you thought you knew me — I'm sorry. This is the only place I've ever told the truth. — P.W.*

---

## Entry I — Colorado Springs

I bought this notebook at the drugstore on Tejon Street, three blocks from the house I grew up in. The man at the register asked if it was for school. I said no. He didn't ask anything else.

I'm twenty years old. I am writing this at the kitchen table of a home where I have never once been hungry, never been cold, never been afraid for my life. My mother is asleep upstairs. My father thinks I'm going through a phase.

This is a city of churches. You can't drive ten minutes without passing a steeple or a billboard or a ministry headquarters. Everyone here knows the words. Everyone sings them on Sunday with their eyes closed and their faces open, and something moves through the room that lifts them, and they go home filled.

I have stood in that room my whole life and felt nothing.

I should write that down plainly, since this notebook is the one place I ever will. When my mother holds me, I feel the pressure of her arms and nothing else. When my father weeps at the Easter vigil, I watch him the way you'd watch rain on a window — I understand what it is, I find it beautiful in a way I can describe but not feel, and it does not touch me. I have never loved anyone. I have never wanted anyone. I have never felt the warmth that every hymn I know is *about.*

Tomorrow I leave. And I am not running from this house, the way a sad person runs. I had a perfect childhood. That's almost the worst part. They gave me everything, and I could not feel a single bit of it. My parents are the only two people on earth who know that about me. Everyone else gets the face I learned very young how to move the right way.

But a boy who cannot feel does something else instead. He *watches.* While everyone around me was busy being swept up — crying, laughing, falling in love, falling apart — I was the still one in the corner, taking it all down. I have spent twenty years studying what feeling does to people, the way a deaf man learns to read the room by the faces. And of everything I watched, one force kept doing things nothing else could. That's what I'm chasing out there. But to explain why a man like me would chase anything at all, I have to start with my mother and father, and what they learned about me a long time ago.

I don't have much of a plan. I keep telling myself I won't need one. I'm calling that faith. I think it might be something else.

## Entry II — The Language I Speak But Cannot Understand

I want to write down how they found out, and what they did, because it is the root of everything and I have never let myself put it in order before.

I was a child when my parents understood that something in me was missing. Not broken in a way that showed — I was bright, too bright, that was half the trouble. I could read before the other children could speak in sentences. By the time I was twelve I had Latin, and the Greek of the Gospels, and the Aramaic my tutor thought was an eccentric thing to want, and of course my own English, and I knew the whole of Scripture so well I could find any verse by the feel of the page. Everyone said I was gifted. They were looking at the part of me that worked.

It was my mother who saw the other part. A mother knows when her child looks at her and the light doesn't come back. There were doctors after that. Tests I was too young to understand and have never forgotten, because I forget nothing — rooms, names, the exact words each specialist used, the particular way my father's face held still while a man explained that his son's capacity for attachment, for warmth, for the felt experience of love, appeared to be absent in a way they could measure but not explain. I remember the word they kept reaching for and discarding. I remember that none of the words quite fit, and that this frightened them more than a clean diagnosis would have.

They tried to fix me. I need to be fair to them here, because they were not cold and this was not neglect — it was the opposite, it was love throwing everything it had at a wall. There were treatments. Things that were supposed to wake the sleeping part. Medicines that did nothing but make the empty quieter. Years of it. And at the end of all of it I was exactly what I had been at the start: a boy who could describe love in four languages and feel it in none.

Here is the thing I have carried every single day since, the thing that made me who I am.

It didn't work — and they loved me anyway.

I want to be clear about what that means, because I have turned it over ten thousand times. They did not love me because I would one day be normal. They did not love me as an investment that might pay out. The treatments failed, the verdict came back final, *your son will not be able to love you the way you love him* — and my mother kept coming into my room every night and sitting on the edge of my bed, and my father kept putting his hand on the back of my neck the way he did, knowing, *knowing* I felt only the weight of it and nothing more. They poured love into a vessel they had been told would never hold it. For nothing. For no return they would ever feel come back.

And I watched them do it, year after year, with my watcher's eyes, and I thought: *that is the most powerful thing I have ever seen a human being do.* To love what cannot love you back. To keep pouring into the dark.

That is when I understood what I was. I am fluent in a language I cannot understand. I can *speak* love — perfectly, more carefully than people who feel it, because I had to learn every word by hand. I can comfort, I can be patient, I can say the thing that mends a person. But I have never understood a single sentence of it spoken *to* me. It goes into my ear as sound. My parents spoke it to me every day of my childhood, beautifully, and I stood there fluent and uncomprehending, like a man who can recite a poem in a tongue he'll never think in.

And then, in the same house, in the same years, I was being taught about Jesus — about a God who pours Himself out for people who cannot love Him back, who keeps coming into the room night after night, who loves the unlovable and the unresponsive and the ones who feel nothing for Him. And I realized I had already *seen* that. I had seen it at the edge of my own bed. My parents had been showing me the gospel with their hands before I had words for it. They loved a son who could not love them, the way God loves a world that does not love Him, and neither of them ever stopped.

I cannot feel that love. I have never felt it. But I am the world's most careful student of it, because I was raised inside a daily demonstration of the exact thing the whole faith is *about* — and I am, perhaps, the one person who had to believe in it without a single feeling to lean on, the way you trust a bridge by its engineering and not by your nerve. Maybe that is why I cannot let it go. It is the only language I am fluent in, even though I will go to my grave never understanding a word.

I'm going to spend my life speaking it to people who can understand it. That seems like the right use of a man who can only speak.

## Entry III — Why a Man Who Feels Nothing Would Do This

I should explain it, because to anyone who knew the truth it would make no sense.

If I cannot feel love, why spend my life on it?

Here is the only answer I have. I cannot feel it — but I have *seen* it. And because I cannot feel, I think I may see it more clearly than the people who can. A man drunk on a thing doesn't study it. I have never been drunk on anything in my life. So I watch. I have spent twenty years doing nothing but watch, and this is what I have seen.

I have a record of it. I have kept one in my head since I was small, the way another boy collects facts about trains.

I saw a man in our parish who beat his family — everyone knew, no one said it — kneel at a rail one Christmas and stand up *different.* Not fixed all at once. But the thing in him that did the harm had been shown something stronger than itself, and over a year I watched it lose. His children stopped flinching when he reached toward them. I cannot tell you what that man felt. I can tell you what I observed, the way you'd chart a fever breaking.

I saw a woman who had buried a child — the kind of grief that kills people, that I am told empties the world of color — and I watched her, a year on, sitting with *another* mother at the start of the same loss, and something passed out of the first woman into the second that held her up over the pit. It came from the church. It came from the words. I have no other name for it than the one they gave me.

I have read what this message did to the whole world, not just our street. Hospitals built where there were none. Slaves walked free because men who owned them read a letter from my namesake and could not square it anymore. Enemies who should have died hating each other, eating at one table. I went looking for the evidence the way a doubter would, half hoping to find it was nothing — and instead I found two thousand years of the dead being raised, sometimes in the plain miraculous way the book describes, and far more often in the slower way, where a wrecked person becomes whole and a cruel one becomes gentle and no one can fully explain it but everyone in the room can see it happened.

I am colorblind to the one color that turns out to matter most. And I have decided that this does not make the color less real. It makes it *more* real to me, somehow — because I have to take it entirely on the evidence of what it does, with none of the feeling clouding my judgment. The people in those rooms might wonder, in a weak hour, whether the warmth they feel is just their own heart fooling them. I never have that doubt. I feel no warmth to be fooled by. I am left with nothing but the results — and the results are the most consistent thing I have ever measured. I believe in love the way a blind astronomer believes in the stars: I will never see them, but I have run the numbers a thousand times, and the numbers do not lie, and I would build telescopes my whole life so that other people can look up.

That is the engine. Not feeling — evidence. I watched what the gospel does to a human being, again and again and again, until the pattern was undeniable. And I thought: *this works. This is the realest thing in the world. And there are people who will live and die having never once been handed it.* I cannot feel how much that matters. But I can count, and the count is unbearable.

When I was a boy, my favorite person in the Bible was Paul of Tarsus. Everyone admired him for being bold. What got me was that he kept *going back* — to people who hated him, for his whole life. He did it, I always assumed, because he was on fire with love.

I am not on fire with anything. There is no fire in me at all. But I read what he did and I thought: *I could do that. I could do the actions even without the fire.* And maybe — this is the thought I keep returning to, the one that frightens me — maybe doing it with no fire, no reward, nothing pulling me forward, is the only way I will ever be sure my love is real and not just a feeling I happened to enjoy having.

> While a person is alive, there is still hope.

That's the whole thing. If everything else in this notebook were stripped away, that's the line I'd keep. I cannot feel hope. But I can refuse to stop. Maybe, for me, those are the same word.

## Entry IV — The Cost Nobody Sees

I need to write down what this actually does to me, because no one else will ever know, and a thing that is never witnessed by anyone starts to feel like it isn't real — even to me, and I forget nothing. I remember every moment of it perfectly. What I'm afraid of losing isn't the memory. It's the *weight.* Say a true thing to no one for long enough and it goes weightless, like it happened to someone else.

People believe I am the kindest person they have ever met. They tell me this. They say I have a gift, that I make them feel seen, that I have more love in me than anyone they know.

Every word of it is built by hand. When I sit with a grieving woman and hold her gaze and say the gentle thing, there is no warmth rising in me to guide the words. There is a cold room inside me and a man in it, working — choosing each sentence, arranging my face, deciding how long to hold her hand based on what I have learned, not what I feel. I am never *moved.* I am always *deciding.* And it is exhausting in a way I have no words gentle enough for. After an hour of being loved by everyone in a room, I have to find somewhere to be alone and sit perfectly still, emptied out, like something burned down to the wire.

But that is not the pain. The exhaustion I could bear forever.

The pain is this: I want it. God help me, I *want* to feel it. I do all of this — every mile, every cold night, every face I memorize — and I am surrounded by the thing I am giving away, and I cannot have any of it. I am a man carrying water across a desert to dying people, and I am dying of the same thirst, and the water will not go down my own throat. I want to feel love so badly that the wanting is the only thing I have ever felt that comes close to a feeling at all. It is an ache with no bottom. It is the single sensation in my entire life, and it is the sensation of lack.

I have never told anyone this. I never will. Because if they knew I felt nothing, they would believe everything I ever gave them was a lie. And it isn't. That's what they'd never understand. It costs me more *because* I feel nothing. It is the most honest thing I have. But I can never say it, so I am saying it here, once, to no one.

## Entry V — The First Lesson Is Always Hunger

I'm not going to pretend I'm not embarrassed writing this.

I thought I understood what it meant to "go without." I did not. I knew the *idea* of hunger the way I knew the idea of the ocean before I ever stood in it.

This is different. I have always had the plain sensations — pressure, heat, cold, the touch of a hand — arriving flat and meaning nothing. But this is the first time my body has handed me something with *urgency* in it, something that grips and demands and won't be set down: hunger that frightens, cold that begs, the animal jolt when footsteps follow me down a dark road. There is one other thing in me that has weight — the ache, the wanting I set down a few pages back — but that one lives somewhere deeper and never lets up. This is different from that. This is loud and bodily and *new,* and my body made it without me, uninvited. I almost welcomed it at first. I lay shivering in a doorway and thought: at least this is real, at least this is mine, at least I did not have to build it by hand.

That is a terrible thing to be grateful for. But I won't cross it out.

I thought trusting God meant I didn't have to think ahead. I'm learning that was never faith — that was me, who feels no fear of consequences because I feel almost nothing at all, mistaking my own flatness for trust. A man who felt things would have been afraid enough to pack water. I wasn't. I have to learn the caution that fear teaches other people for free. I have to learn it from the outside, like everything else.

## Entry VI — The Bubble

Here is the thing I am most ashamed of: I knew *everything* and I knew *nothing.*

I have memorized scripture. I can hold my own in any debate about doctrine. I walked out into the world genuinely believing my words were ready.

My words were built for a room that doesn't exist out here. The people I'm meeting don't want to debate. A man this week, when I started to speak, just looked at me — not angry, just tired — and asked if I had food, and when I said no, he stopped listening. And he was *right* to. My beautiful theology had nothing to offer a man who hadn't eaten.

And I keep making it worse in a way that is particular to me. When my approach fails, a person with feelings would *sense* the room turning, would feel the discomfort and stop. I don't feel the room. I have to read it from the outside — the tightening of a jaw, the half-step backward, the eyes going flat — decoding it piece by piece while a feeling person already knows in their gut. I read too slowly, and so I keep pushing the broken thing long after their instinct would have screamed at them to stop. I have hurt people this way. My missing feeling, which I have always treated as my private wound, turns out to wound *others* too. That is new. I had thought I was the only one who paid for what I am.

## Entry VII — The People Who Are Teaching Me

The people teaching me the most out here are not the people I came to "save." Or — they are. But it's backwards from how I imagined it.

There's a man I won't name yet. He's done things, real things, the kind I was raised to be afraid of. And in three days he taught me more about staying alive, reading a stranger's face, *when to shut my mouth,* than my entire upbringing did.

He said something I keep turning over. He watched me work a crowd — watched me be gentle and patient and good with a difficult woman — and afterward he looked at me a long time and said, "You're not in there, are you." Not cruel. Just true. He's the only one who has ever seen it. He doesn't know what he saw. But he saw that the man everyone was loving wasn't *home* behind his own eyes.

I should have been frightened. Instead I felt the ache come up — the wanting — because for one second someone had looked straight through the performance and not flinched. And I thought: maybe the people I came to save are the only ones who could ever stand to know what I am. The respectable people need me to be the saint. He doesn't need anything from me. He could know the truth and still sit with me. That is closer to being loved than anything I have ever almost-had.

## Entry VIII — About Dying

I'm not brave. When someone threatens me — and it has happened — my hands shake, because the body shakes on its own; that much I get for free.

But the fear isn't shaped the way it is for other people. I don't lie awake thinking *I might die.* Here is the truth I can only write here: there isn't much in this life I would grieve losing. There is no joy in it for me to leave behind. No warmth, no love, no pleasure waiting in my tomorrows that death would steal. In a way I will not say out loud to anyone, death has never frightened me the way it should, because I have never been given the thing that makes people cling to living.

What I'm afraid of is dying *with the work unfinished.* Someone I started and didn't get back to. A name I memorized and failed to return for. That is the only death that frightens me — not mine, but the people on the other side of mine.

And under that, tonight, a worse thought. My whole life the Church told me salvation is God's work, that I am only an instrument. So why am I terrified that someone will be lost because I didn't reach them in time? If I believed what I was taught, that fear would be impossible. My panic only makes sense if I have quietly started to believe it all depends on me — that I am not the instrument but the rescuer. That isn't humility. That's pride wearing humility's clothes.

But maybe a man who feels nothing has to believe it depends on him. Maybe that's the only engine I have. A normal believer is carried along by the joy of the thing. I have no joy to be carried by. If I don't believe every soul hangs on me personally, I have *no reason at all* to get up off the cold ground tomorrow. So I tell myself a lie that keeps me walking. I don't know yet whether God forgives a lie like that, or whether He gave it to me on purpose.

## Entry IX — Whether Gentleness Is Always Love

Something happened this week I can't shake.

I sat with a woman whose husband drinks and then uses his hands on her and the children. I did what I always do. I was gentle. I was patient. I built the kind words carefully and laid them down one at a time.

An older woman from the same street pulled me aside afterward, *furious.* She said gentleness was the last thing that house needed from a man — that my soft words let everyone keep pretending, that what was needed was someone willing to make a hard, ugly scene, to stand between him and them, to be *feared* a little. She said I'd mistaken being nice for being good, and they were not the same, and my kind of love would get someone hurt while I felt holy about it.

She doesn't know the joke in it. I didn't feel holy. I didn't feel anything. I had simply *chosen* gentleness, the way I choose everything — from a menu of approaches, picking the one that has worked before. And here is what shook me: because I run on calculation instead of feeling, I had quietly assumed my calculations were *cleaner* than other people's messy emotions. Wiser. Less biased. She just proved I was wrong. A mother on that street, full of the very feelings I lack, read the danger in one second that my careful cold machinery missed completely.

My namesake wasn't gentle. He raged, named names, drew blood with words when he thought blood was what love required. I read those passages as a boy and was embarrassed for him. I'm starting to understand. Sometimes the fiercest thing in the room is the loving one. I chose softness because softness is *easy to perform* — it asks nothing of me but patience, and patience is the one thing emptiness is good at. Real love might have demanded I feel enough rage to stand and fight. And I had none to give.

## Entry X — The Thing I Can't Resolve

I'll write down the contradiction I live inside, since it isn't going anywhere.

I believe a person's freedom has to be respected. I will not force anyone to accept a love they don't want. And yet I also believe every single life is worth the attempt. Every one. No exceptions. These two things don't fit together cleanly, and I've stopped pretending they do. So I keep going. I keep coming back. I remember the names, the families, the small things people said when they thought no one was listening — and remembering, for me, is not warmth, it is *discipline,* a thing I do on purpose because I decided each person is worth the effort of being kept.

Some of them love me for it. Some find me unbearable. I understand both, from the outside.

But the contradiction under all the others is the one I can't write anywhere but here. I have given my whole life to making people feel a thing I will never feel. And I do not know what that means. Some nights I think it is the dark night the mystics wrote about — that God has taken away all consolation and left me only the bare will to love, and that this stripped-down, joyless, reward-less love is the *purest* kind, closer to the cross than anyone with feelings could ever come. On those nights it almost seems like a gift shaped like a wound.

Other nights I think there is no meaning in it at all. That I am simply broken — that some wire was never connected, that it is chemistry and not calling, and that I have built a cathedral of purpose on top of a defect to keep myself from despair. That I am not a saint in darkness. Just a damaged man who found a beautiful use for his damage.

I go back and forth. I have never landed. I don't think I'm meant to.

> You can reject me today. You can reject me tomorrow. You can reject me for ten years. But as long as you're alive, I won't lose hope.

I cannot feel hope. I want that understood, here, where it's safe to say it. When I tell them I won't lose hope, what I mean is: I have decided not to stop, and I will keep that decision long after every feeling that should support it has failed to arrive. Maybe that's a lesser thing than real hope.

Or maybe it's the most that love has ever asked of anyone — to keep going with nothing underneath you but the choice itself.

I don't know which. I'm not the boy who bought this notebook on Tejon Street, the one who thought feeling nothing was something he'd hide forever. I still hide it. But at least now I know what it costs, and who it costs, and I've stopped pretending the cost is only mine.

There is one thing I hold onto, on the nights when I decide it's a defect and not a calling. My parents loved a boy who could never love them back. They got nothing for it, not once, in twenty years — and they would tell you it was worth every day. If their love was real while pouring into someone who couldn't receive it, then maybe mine is real while pouring out of someone who can't feel it. They are the proof I was raised inside. Whatever I am, I learned it watching two people refuse to stop loving the dark — and call it the best thing they ever did.

So I'll speak the language. I'll speak it to everyone, fluently, beautifully, to my last breath, in the four tongues I was given and the one I never will be. I will never understand a word of it said back to me. But I have decided that doesn't matter. A man can carry a message in a language he doesn't feel, the way a letter carries words the courier never reads. I am the courier. The message is true whether or not the one carrying it can read his own cargo.

I'm not ready. I'm just here. And for a man like me, choosing to still be here, choosing it again every single morning with nothing pulling me up off the ground — maybe that's the closest I will ever come to love.

I'll take it. It's what I have.

— P.W.
